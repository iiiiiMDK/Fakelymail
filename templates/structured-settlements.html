Structured Settlements: A Smart Financial Choice After Legal Claims

Introduction

Structured settlements are a financial arrangement where individuals receive compensation from a legal claim through scheduled payments over time, rather than in a single lump sum. They are commonly used in personal injury, wrongful death, medical malpractice, and product liability cases. This method of compensation helps ensure long-term financial stability for claimants and protects against the risk of mismanaging a large one-time payout. In this article, we'll cover how structured settlements work, their advantages and disadvantages, how they're used, tax considerations, and what to consider if you're thinking about selling your future payments.

How Structured Settlements Work

When a legal case concludes with a structured settlement, the defendant (or their insurance company) agrees to fund the settlement by purchasing an annuity from a life insurance company. The annuity is designed to issue periodic payments to the claimant based on a pre-agreed schedule. These payments can be monthly, annually, or set up in a customized structure depending on the recipient’s needs. For example, a recipient might receive increasing payments over time, lump sums at key intervals (such as when children reach college age), or lifelong income for permanent injuries.

Advantages of Structured Settlements

Structured settlements offer several significant benefits. First, they provide guaranteed income over time, offering peace of mind to recipients who may be unable to return to work or need support for ongoing medical expenses. Second, the interest earned on the annuity is tax-free if the settlement stems from a personal physical injury claim. This makes structured settlements an attractive option for claimants who want to avoid large tax liabilities. Third, because the money comes in periodic payments, it reduces the temptation to overspend or make poor financial decisions, protecting long-term financial well-being.

Tax Benefits and Implications

Under the Internal Revenue Code (IRC) Section 104(a)(2), payments received from structured settlements related to personal injuries or physical sickness are exempt from federal and most state income taxes. However, investment income generated from reinvesting those payments may be taxable. If a structured settlement is modified or sold, there may also be tax consequences depending on the terms of the sale and the type of buyer. It’s important to work with a tax professional to understand the full implications before making changes to your settlement.

Common Use Cases

Structured settlements are commonly used in legal cases involving severe injury, permanent disability, or wrongful death. They are also favored when the claimant is a minor, providing income or lump sums at milestone ages like 18, 21, or 25. Structured settlements are particularly useful for individuals who need consistent income over time to manage long-term medical costs or living expenses. In some cases, judges mandate structured settlements to ensure the funds are not mismanaged, especially when claimants are considered financially vulnerable.

Structured Settlements for Minors

When a legal case involves a child, courts often require that the settlement be structured to ensure the funds are protected until the child reaches adulthood. Typically, a structured annuity is created that issues payments at set ages—often starting at 18—with increases as the child reaches maturity. This protects the child from accessing large sums too early, reducing the risk of financial mismanagement and ensuring funds are available for college, housing, or medical needs.

Disadvantages and Risks

Despite their many benefits, structured settlements have limitations. Once a payment plan is established, it’s generally irrevocable. This means recipients can't access a larger amount of money in emergencies unless they sell part of the settlement, which often results in a loss of value. Additionally, inflation can erode the purchasing power of fixed payments unless the annuity includes a cost-of-living adjustment (COLA). Another concern is the financial strength of the life insurance company issuing the annuity—if the company fails, future payments could be jeopardized.

Selling Structured Settlements

Sometimes, recipients of structured settlements consider selling part or all of their future payments in exchange for a lump sum of cash. This may be necessary during emergencies or to fund major life expenses like buying a home, starting a business, or paying off debt. Selling structured settlements requires court approval to ensure the transaction is in the best interest of the seller. Judges often consider the seller’s financial situation, reasoning for the sale, and understanding of the consequences.

How the Sale Process Works

To sell a structured settlement, the seller contacts a factoring company, which provides a quote for purchasing the future payments. If the offer is accepted, legal paperwork is filed, and a hearing is scheduled where a judge evaluates the proposed sale. If approved, the factoring company pays the seller the agreed lump sum and receives the future payments instead. Many sellers don’t realize they’ll receive significantly less than the total future value of the settlement—often only 50–70%—so it’s critical to compare multiple offers and seek legal or financial advice.

Court Approval Requirements

Structured settlement sales are subject to both state and federal laws to protect sellers from exploitation. Courts typically require disclosure statements that show the present value of the payments, the discount rate applied, and the financial impact of the sale. Judges want to be sure that the seller is making an informed decision and that the sale won’t leave them financially unstable in the future. Some states even require a cooling-off period where the seller can reconsider their decision after signing.

Choosing a Factoring Company

When selecting a factoring company to purchase structured settlement payments, it’s essential to evaluate the company's reputation, fees, transparency, and customer service. Look for companies that are licensed in your state, offer competitive discount rates, and provide all terms in writing. Avoid any company that pressures you into a quick decision or downplays the long-term impact of selling your payments. Reading reviews and consulting with a lawyer can help ensure you’re working with a reputable firm.

Alternatives to Selling

If you’re considering selling your settlement due to financial hardship, consider alternatives first. Government programs, personal loans, budgeting assistance, or nonprofit grants might offer temporary relief. Additionally, some insurance companies allow partial buyouts or payment adjustments without full sale. Exploring all other options ensures you make the most informed and financially sound decision for your situation.

Conclusion

Structured settlements are a powerful tool that provides long-term financial stability to individuals recovering from trauma, injury, or loss. While they offer many advantages—such as tax-free payments and income security—they also come with restrictions that require careful consideration. Whether you’re receiving a structured settlement, managing one, or considering selling your future payments, be sure to consult professionals and fully understand your rights, risks, and opportunities. Making the right decision today can ensure peace of mind and financial health for years to come.
